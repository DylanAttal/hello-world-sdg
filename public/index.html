How I got into Brazilian Jiu Jitsu

Growing up, I never much enjoyed sports. My parents threw me into every sport with a team and a ball, but none of them stuck. I ended up swimming competitively for more than a decade, though, simply because it was the sport that my brother loved and it was easy for my mom to drop us off at the same practice. Did I care if I got beat in a race? Absolutely not. But it kept me in shape and gave me a nice tan, so I swam.

Fast forward to college graduation. I moved back to my hometown of Tampa, and I needed a way to stay fit. I had long since stopped swimming, and returning to the pool was not an option. Going to the gym alone was so boring. I craved a new experience as I embarked on a new chapter in my life. This was during the heyday of Ronda Rousey, a Judo-trained UFC fighter who went two and a half years undefeated and who holds the record as the longest reigning UFC Women's Bantamweight Champion. By chance, I saw her fight while I was out for drinks at a bar. She was incredible. The short, beautiful blonde had everyone in the bar standing, applauding, and yelling. I've never been excited for watching a sports match before. I go for the wings and beer. But that night I was pounding the table like everyone else, and when that fervor took me by surprise, I thought I've got to learn how to do that.

I'm a bookworm. I'm a nerd trapped in an athlete's body. I had literally never gotten into a physical altercation before--not even a scuffle at lunch in middle school. So suffice it to say I was nervous walking into a martial arts studio for the first time. But I had decided to learn a new skill, and I'm nothing if not determined. Luckily, I lived near a studio with an amazing sensei. Ryan Deichert is a Gracie Jiu Jitsu Black Belt who studied under the legendary Royce Gracie and opened his own studio in Tampa. He's a down-to-earth, humble guy who you'd never suspect could rip you apart like a grizzly bear. The students at Tampa Jiu Jitsu were similarly welcoming, and despite getting tapped by literal high-schoolers every night, I persevered in my training. Before I knew it a half a year had passed, everyone knew my name, and I had found a community.
